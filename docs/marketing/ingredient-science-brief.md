### Point 1 — The Ingredient List Is a Ranking, Not a Random List

**The fact:**
By law (both EU and US), cosmetic and food ingredient lists must be written in order from highest concentration to lowest — down to the 1% threshold. The first ingredient is the biggest component of what you're putting on your body. The third ingredient is still a major part of the formula.

**Why it's alarming:**
Most people read ingredient lists like a flat list of equal things. They're not. When Sodium Lauryl Sulfate — a known skin and eye irritant — is the third ingredient in a product, it's not a trace amount. It's a core part of the formula. The list is telling you something specific, but nobody taught you how to read it.

**The ManGood angle:**
ManGood weighs ingredients by their position. An irritant at position 2 hits differently than an irritant at position 12. Our scoring reflects concentration, not just presence.

**Suggested content formats:**
- Instagram carousel: "How to actually read an ingredient list" (swipe-through education)
- Short-form video: hold up a shaving cream, point to ingredient #3
- Email subject line: "The label is telling you something. Here's how to read it."
- Ad hook: "Most men read ingredient labels wrong. Here's what position #3 actually means."

### Point 2 — One Word on the Label Can Hide Hundreds of Chemicals

**The fact:**
"Fragrance" or "Parfum" is a single INCI (International Nomenclature of Cosmetic Ingredients) entry that legally conceals an entire proprietary formula — potentially hundreds of individual chemicals. Manufacturers are not required to disclose what's inside it. Common hidden components include phthalates (anti-androgenic endocrine disruptors), synthetic musks (hormone-disrupting), and documented allergens. The EU recently expanded its list of fragrance allergens that must be declared separately to over 80 compounds — but only for products made after 2025.

**Why it's alarming:**
You are literally buying a product where one ingredient is a black box. A brand can put a hormone-disrupting chemical into a body wash and list it as "Parfum." You have no way of knowing it's there unless you know to look for it — and even then, the label won't say. This is not a conspiracy; it is the law.

**The ManGood angle:**
ManGood flags "Parfum" as a concern in every grooming product that contains it, with a plain-English explanation of what the hidden risk is. You deserve to know what you're not being told.

**Suggested content formats:**
- Hook post: "This one word on your shampoo bottle can legally hide 200 chemicals."
- Infographic: The Parfum Black Box — what might be inside (phthalates, synthetic musks, allergens, etc.)
- Email: "Your aftershave probably contains this. You just can't see it on the label."
- Ad creative: show an ingredient list zoomed in on "Parfum" with a red flag

### Point 3 — Your Aftershave Sits on Your Skin All Day. Your Face Wash Doesn't.

**The fact:**
Regulatory bodies (EU's Scientific Committee on Consumer Safety, the US Cosmetic Ingredient Review) set different safe concentration limits for the same ingredient depending on whether a product washes off or stays on your skin. A rinse-off product like a shampoo or face wash contacts your skin for seconds. A leave-on product like an aftershave balm, moisturiser, or deodorant is in contact for hours — with full dermal absorption. The same ingredient that's acceptable in a shampoo can be a genuine concern in an aftershave.

**Why it's alarming:**
Your grooming routine probably includes both. But you're likely evaluating products as if they're equal. The aftershave you splash on after every shave — absorbed by freshly shaved, microabrasion-irritated skin — is the one that deserves the most scrutiny. Most men spend more time choosing a razor than checking what's in the product that goes on directly after.

**The ManGood angle:**
ManGood treats grooming products with awareness of how they're used. Leave-on products are held to a higher standard. We're building toward product-type intelligence so scores reflect actual exposure, not just ingredient presence.

**Suggested content formats:**
- Educational post: "Rinse-off vs leave-on — the grooming distinction your brand won't tell you about"
- Email: "You shave. Then you put this directly on broken skin. We looked at what's in it."
- Content series: "The Daily Exposure Series" — break down the average man's morning routine product by product
- Podcast talking point / talking head video: explain the CIR leave-on standard in plain language

### Point 4 — Some Reactions Get Worse Every Time. Not Better.

**The fact:**
There are two types of ingredient reactions: irritation and sensitisation. Irritation is dose-dependent and reversible — it affects most people at high enough concentrations and stops when exposure stops. Sensitisation is different. It's an acquired immune response — your body learns to react to a specific chemical, and once it does, even trace amounts trigger a reaction. It gets worse with each exposure, not better. Common sensitisers in grooming products include fragrance components, methylisothiazolinone (a preservative), and certain dyes. Once you're sensitised, you're sensitised for life.

**Why it's alarming:**
The man who develops a contact allergy to a fragrance component in his aftershave didn't do anything wrong. He used a product sold to him as normal. By the time he notices something is wrong — months or years of exposure later — the sensitisation is already complete. No one warned him. No one told him that repeated use of this ingredient was building up a biological response that cannot be undone.

**The ManGood angle:**
ManGood flags confirmed sensitisers separately. We treat them differently from irritants because the risk profile is different — no safe threshold applies once sensitisation occurs. This isn't fearmongering. It's information you needed before the first use, not after the hundredth.

**Suggested content formats:**
- "Things that get worse, not better" — a counter-intuitive hook for social
- Long-form email: tell the story of sensitisation as a timeline ("Day 1: nothing. Month 6: mild reaction. Year 2: can't use anything with fragrance.")
- FAQ post: "Is my skin getting used to this product, or is something else happening?"
- This is strong podcast / interview content — the "you can't un-sensitise" fact is genuinely shocking to most people

### Point 5 — There's a Carcinogen in Your Shampoo That Isn't Listed on the Label

**The fact:**
1,4-dioxane is classified as a probable human carcinogen by the US EPA and the IARC (International Agency for Research on Cancer, Group 2B). It is not an ingredient — it is a manufacturing contaminant that forms as a byproduct during a process called ethoxylation, which is used to make certain surfactants and emulsifiers milder. Any ingredient with "eth" in its name (Sodium Laureth Sulfate, Laureth-23, Ceteareth-20) or starting with "PEG-" can contain trace amounts. Because it's a contaminant rather than an added ingredient, manufacturers are not required to list it. It has been detected in mainstream shampoos, body washes, and baby products in multiple independent lab analyses.

**Why it's alarming:**
This is not a hypothetical. Independent testing labs (EWG, Consumer Reports) have detected 1,4-dioxane in products sold at every major retailer. The company isn't hiding it on purpose — it forms in the manufacturing process and isn't tested for on the finished product unless someone specifically looks. But the result is the same: you are applying a probable carcinogen to your scalp several times a week and there is no regulatory requirement for anyone to tell you.

**The ManGood angle:**
ManGood flags every ethoxylated ingredient it detects with a caution and explains why. We can't test the product ourselves, but we can tell you that the ingredient pathway exists and what it means. That's more than the label is telling you.

**Suggested content formats:**
- This is the highest-impact hook in the entire brief. Lead with it.
- "There is a probable carcinogen in your shampoo. It's not listed." — social hook
- Email subject: "The ingredient that isn't on the label."
- Deep-dive blog post: what ethoxylation is, what 1,4-dioxane is, which ingredients to watch for
- Visual: show ingredient list with PEG-40, Laureth-23, SLES highlighted with a "trace contaminant pathway" callout
- This is shareable enough to be a standalone viral piece without selling anything

### Point 6 — Your Shower Gel + Your Moisturiser + Your Deodorant = Nobody Knows

**The fact:**
Every safety assessment for every ingredient is done in isolation — on a single product, at a defined concentration, with a defined use pattern. No regulatory body currently assesses the aggregate exposure from a person's full daily routine. The average person uses 10–15 personal care products per day. If three of those products each contain a low-level irritant that is individually "within safe limits," the combined daily exposure has never been formally assessed. Scientists call this the "cocktail effect" and it is an acknowledged gap in consumer safety regulation worldwide.

**Why it's alarming:**
The safety label on any single product tells you that product is safe in isolation. It tells you nothing about what happens when you stack it with everything else you use. Nobody is doing that calculation for you. Not the brand. Not the regulator. Nobody.

**The ManGood angle:**
ManGood is building toward whole-routine scoring — not just individual products, but the aggregate picture of what you're applying to your body in a given day. For now, we flag repeat exposure risks across your scan history so you can see patterns the product-level view misses.

**Suggested content formats:**
- "Your morning routine is an experiment. Here's what's in it." — strong content series hook
- Routine audit content: take a real morning routine (user-submitted or constructed) and score every product
- Infographic: "The average man's daily ingredient load" — aggregate count of flagged ingredients across a typical 7-product morning
- This is strong editorial/thought leadership content for earned media

### Point 7 — pH Adjusters Can Become Carcinogens. Depending on What They're Mixed With.

**The fact:**
Triethanolamine (TEA) is a common pH adjuster in shaving creams, face washes, and lotions. On its own, it is moderately concerning. But when combined with certain other ingredients — specifically DEA (diethanolamine) derivatives or formaldehyde-releasing preservatives — it undergoes a chemical reaction that forms N-nitrosamines, a family of compounds classified as probable carcinogens. The EU has restricted TEA's use in products that also contain nitrosating agents for this reason. Most consumers have no idea that two separately "acceptable" ingredients can combine into something of greater concern.

**Why it's alarming:**
The ingredient list doesn't tell you about reactions between ingredients. A brand can put TEA and a DEA compound in the same product, and each one, looked at individually, passes review. The combination is the problem — and that combination only becomes visible when you know to look for it. This is not a regulatory failure; it is simply a gap between what the label communicates and what the chemistry actually describes.

**The ManGood angle:**
ManGood flags TEA as a caution in all grooming products and explains the nitrosamine formation pathway in plain language. Future versions will detect co-ingredient risk patterns — flagging the TEA harder when a known nitrosating agent is also present.

**Suggested content formats:**
- "Some ingredients are dangerous alone. Some are dangerous together." — concept for a content series
- "The chemistry your label doesn't show you" — explainer format
- This is good long-form content: not immediately alarming on its own, but part of a broader "the label doesn't tell the whole story" narrative

## Constraints for the AI Agent

- **Do not sensationalise beyond the facts stated.** Every claim above is backed by a regulatory body (EU, EPA, IARC, CIR, SCCS) or peer-reviewed literature. Do not extend claims beyond what is written.
- **Do not use the word "toxic."** It is vague, legally risky, and erodes credibility. Use precise language: carcinogen, endocrine disruptor, sensitiser, irritant.
- **Do not claim ManGood eliminates risk.** It surfaces information. The user makes the decision.
- **Avoid fear without resolution.** Every alarming point must end with ManGood as the actionable response — not anxiety, but control.
- **Preferred voice:** Knowledgeable friend. Not alarmist. Not clinical. Direct, human, confident.
